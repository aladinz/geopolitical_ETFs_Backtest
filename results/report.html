01 Purpose & Scope

This report examines how six sector ETFs — XLE energy, ITA PPA defense, JETS airlines, QQQ technology, and SPY broad market — have responded to major geopolitical and oil-related shocks between 2001 and 2026. The analysis is grounded in real, tradeable ETF price data. Events were selected because they produced measurable disruptions to oil supply, regional security, or investor risk appetite. The goal is to surface recurring behavioural tendencies across events, not to construct a predictive model. The dataset covers seven events; the patterns observed are directional guides, not statistically conclusive findings.

02 Sector Patterns

A consistent sector hierarchy emerges in the initial shock window. XLE tends to rise when oil-supply routes or production infrastructure are perceived to be at risk. ITA and PPA show steady gains in the weeks following escalation events, reflecting expectations of increased defence procurement. JETS is the most vulnerable sector, falling sharply when fuel costs spike or travel demand is threatened. QQQ dips at the initial shock but recovers quickly unless recession risk rises materially. SPY is the baseline: a short pullback followed by stabilisation is the norm in most cases.

03 Duration & Consistency

The strongest and most consistent reactions occur in the T+1 to T+5 window — the first week after a shock. Beyond that, mean reversion is the dominant pattern. Most events do not create sustained multi-month trends; the market processes the shock, prices in a new baseline, and carries forward. The exceptions are notable: shocks that physically threaten oil supply over a prolonged period, or that escalate into a sustained military campaign, tend to extend energy and defence outperformance into the T+60 window. Contained events — a single exchange, a brief escalation — show faster reversion across all sectors.

04 Interpreting New Shocks

This data supports a simple framing: energy and defence typically carry the bid; airlines and broad risk assets typically carry the fear. The speed of reversion depends on whether the shock remains contained or escalates into something with tangible economic consequences. That distinction — contained versus structural — is the most important judgement to make when a new headline arrives, and it is one this model cannot make for you.

    Limitations & Integrity Methodological transparency

    This section documents the constraints, assumptions, and sources of uncertainty built into this backtest. Reading it is not optional — it is part of understanding the output.

    Limitation 01
    📅 ETF Data Availability
    Most of the ETFs in this report did not exist before the early 2000s. XLE launched in 1998, ITA in 2001, PPA in 2005, JETS in 2015, QQQ in 1999. This means the dataset cannot directly analyse the 1973 OPEC embargo, the 1979 Iranian revolution, the 1990 Gulf War, or the 2003 SARS shock — all of which produced significant energy and equity dislocations. This report focuses exclusively on post-2000 events because that is where real, tradeable ETF price data exists. Any pattern claimed here is derived from that narrower window, not from the full history of geopolitical shocks.
    Limitation 02
    🪟 Event Window Assumptions
    The windows used — T+1, T+5, T+20, T+60 — are reasonable conventions, not universal truths. A different choice of window (say T+3 or T+45) would produce different numbers. More importantly, markets frequently price in geopolitical risk before the official event date — through elevated option implied volatility, pre-positioning in energy futures, or sector rotation. The T0 anchor is the first trading day on or after the acknowledged event date, which may already reflect partial pricing. Results are therefore sensitive to definition, and should not be read as precision measurements.
    Limitation 03
    🌍 Heterogeneity of Shocks
    The seven events in this dataset are not the same type of shock. 9/11 was a domestic terrorism event with a massive demand-side impact on aviation. The Iraq War was a pre-announced military campaign with known timelines. The Gaza and Iran events were regional conflicts with limited direct economic linkage to the US. Aggregating them into a single summary statistic creates noise and dilutes signal. Win rates and averages across heterogeneous events should be interpreted with caution — they describe a distribution, not a reliable mechanism.
    Limitation 04
    🛢️ Oil Price Attribution
    Oil prices move for many reasons that have nothing to do with geopolitics: OPEC+ production decisions, US shale supply growth, global demand cycles, the US dollar index, and speculative flow all drive XLE and USO returns. When this model records an XLE return in the weeks after an event, it cannot cleanly separate the geopolitical risk premium from concurrent macro forces. An XLE decline observed after the Iraq War, for example, partly reflected a post-shock demand recovery — not a refutation of the energy-shock thesis. Attribution is inherently ambiguous without a controlled counterfactual.
    Limitation 05
    🏗️ Market Structure Changes
    The US energy sector in 2001 and in 2026 are structurally different markets. The shale revolution turned the US from a net oil importer to the world's largest producer, changing the transmission mechanism between Middle East supply shocks and domestic energy equities. Similarly, the defense industrial base has consolidated, and ETF index construction has evolved. Historical reactions from 2001–2006 may systematically understate or overstate the response modern markets would generate to an equivalent shock, because the underlying economic relationships have shifted.
    Limitation 06
    🗂️ Data Quality & Survivorship
    All ETF data in this report is sourced from Yahoo Finance via yfinance and reflects adjusted closing prices accounting for splits and dividends. However, ETF index methodologies change over time — constituents are rebalanced, weighting schemes evolve, and expense ratios shift. JETS, for example, reconstituted its index methodology in 2020 following COVID-related delistings. Older price history may reflect a different portfolio composition than today's fund. This introduces a subtle form of index drift that the model does not correct for.
    Limitation 07
    🚫 No Predictive Claims
    This report makes no predictions. Every number presented — average returns, win rates, alpha, Sharpe ratios — is a description of what happened in a small sample of historical events. Past patterns in a dataset of seven events do not constitute a statistically reliable forecast of future outcomes. Geopolitical shocks are, by definition, low-frequency, high-impact events with fat-tailed outcome distributions. The confidence interval around any estimate here is wide enough to overlap with the opposite conclusion. This analysis reveals tendencies and mechanisms — it does not provide trading signals or investment advice.
    A note on intellectual honesty. The purpose of this section is not to undermine confidence in the analysis — it is to calibrate it correctly. A backtest built on seven events, using ETFs that are at most 25 years old, applied to shocks that differ fundamentally in type and magnitude, can still be genuinely useful. It surfaces behavioural patterns, directional tendencies, and relative sector dynamics that are difficult to see in real time. What it cannot do is tell you with certainty what will happen next. The reader who understands both what this model says and what it cannot say is better positioned than one who treats either the numbers or the caveats in isolation.
    Shock Playbook Recurring patterns across geopolitical events

    Every geopolitical shock arrives with its own headline, geography, and cast of actors. But beneath the noise, a small set of recurring structural patterns emerge across the events in this dataset. This playbook summarises those tendencies — the sectors that consistently catch a bid, the sectors that consistently absorb fear, and the speed at which markets typically digest each shock type. These are observed tendencies, not predictions. Each new crisis carries unique drivers that historical averages cannot fully anticipate.

When a headline appears, the first question to ask is: which shock type does this most resemble? That framing helps you understand which sectors are likely carrying the fear, which are likely catching a bid, and over what time horizon historical patterns suggest resolution. Use it to ask better questions, not to place orders.
